Glossary of Windstorm Terms

Below you will find definitions and explanations of common terms used when discussing windstorms.

Windstorm
A storm with very strong winds but little or no rain, strong enough to cause damage. (Source: Wikipedia)
Extratropical Cyclone
A low-pressure weather system that forms outside the tropics and is associated with strong winds and frontal boundaries. These are the systems that produce most European windstorms. (Source: Wikipedia)
Jet Stream
A fast-flowing air current in the upper atmosphere that influences storm development and movement across Europe. (Source: Wikipedia)
Pressure Gradient
The rate of pressure change between two locations. A steeper gradient means stronger winds. (Source: Britannica)
Gale
A wind with speeds between 62–74 km/h (39–46 mph) on the Beaufort scale. Often associated with strong low-pressure systems. (Source: Wikipedia)
Beaufort Scale
A scale for estimating wind speed based on observed sea conditions or land effects, ranging from 0 (calm) to 12 (hurricane). (Source: Britannica)
Downburst
A powerful downdraft from a thunderstorm that hits the ground and spreads out in all directions, causing damaging winds. (Source: Wikipedia)
Storm Surge
A rise in sea level caused by strong winds and low pressure from a storm pushing water toward the coast. (Source: NOAA)
Squall Line
A line of thunderstorms that can produce strong straight-line winds, heavy rain, and sometimes tornadoes. (Source: Wikipedia)
Occluded Front
A weather front formed when a cold front overtakes a warm front, common in mature extratropical cyclones. (Source: Met Office)
Anticyclone
A high-pressure system associated with calm, clear weather and light winds. (Source: Wikipedia)
Synoptic Chart
A weather map showing pressure systems, fronts, and weather observations at a specific time. (Source: Met Office)
